Abstract
Cameroon Post (CAMPOST) maintains a well-established national postal network that serves millions of customers across Cameroon. However, the ability to track parcels across the national territory in real time, and to integrate such tracking with digital payment and automated customer notification, has remained limited. This dissertation presents the design and complete implementation of SmartCAMPOST — a multi-platform, AI-enhanced intelligent parcel tracking, collection, and delivery management system conceived as a complement to CAMPOST's existing services.
A bilingual needs-assessment survey administered in English and French via Google Forms to 30 respondents — CAMPOST clients, agents, and couriers in Yaoundé — confirmed strong demand for real-time tracking (4.7/5), mobile payment integration (4.6/5), and SMS notifications (4.5/5). A hybrid methodology combining Merise data modelling and Agile Scrum iterative development was employed across twelve development sprints.
The system comprises a Spring Boot 3.5.7 RESTful backend, a React 19 TypeScript web portal, and a Flutter 3.11.1 Android mobile application. The database encompasses 45 tables hosted on TiDB Cloud. Seven user roles are enforced with role-based access control at the API level. Key capabilities include dual QR code parcel identification, GPS real-time tracking, a multi-agent AI framework (Courier Agent, Agency Agent, Risk Agent) powered by OpenAI GPT, and mobile payment processing through MTN Mobile Money, Orange Money, and CAMPOST Money.
Automated testing achieved a 99.4% pass rate across 170 test cases. All four bugs identified during structured manual end-to-end testing were diagnosed and resolved. The system was deployed on Render (backend), Vercel (web), and distributed as a signed Android APK via GitHub Releases v1.0.0.
Keywords: Parcel Tracking, QR Code, GPS, Artificial Intelligence, Spring Boot, Flutter, Mobile Payment, CAMPOST, Cameroon, Postal Digitalisation.

CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION
1. Introduction
In the rapidly evolving landscape of digital logistics and postal services, intelligent parcel tracking systems have become a hallmark of efficient and customer-centric operations. SmartCAMPOST is an innovative multi-platform system that seeks to revolutionize parcel management in Cameroon by connecting clients, agents, couriers, and administrators through a unified digital platform powered by QR codes, GPS real-time tracking, and Artificial Intelligence. This application is designed to seamlessly integrate with the operational environment of Cameroon Post (CAMPOST) — the national postal operator — adding digital capabilities that complement and extend its well-established physical infrastructure.
The platform addresses the needs of a diverse set of users: clients who send and receive parcels, agents who handle intake and transit scanning, couriers who manage last-mile delivery, and administrators who oversee the entire system. With features including bilingual support (English and French), mobile payment integration via MTN Mobile Money and Orange Money, automated SMS notifications, and an AI multi-agent framework for delivery prediction and route optimisation, SmartCAMPOST brings world-class postal logistics technology to the Cameroonian context. SmartCAMPOST is not merely a tracking application — it is a gateway to a more transparent, efficient, and inclusive postal future for Cameroon.
1.1  Background to the Problem
The postal system has long constituted a fundamental pillar of communication, commerce, and social cohesion across the world. In Cameroon, the Cameroun Postal Services — commonly known as CAMPOST or La Poste du Cameroun — operates a national network of agencies and distribution centres responsible for the collection, transit, and delivery of parcels and correspondence to individuals, businesses, and public institutions throughout the country. CAMPOST represents a strategic public infrastructure with the potential to serve as a backbone for inclusive digital commerce in Cameroon.
CAMPOST has invested meaningfully in expanding its physical agency presence across both urban centres and semi-rural areas. The institution provides a range of services including domestic parcel handling, financial transfers, and various public service operations. Like many postal operators in the developing world, however, CAMPOST faces the challenge of adapting its service delivery model to the expectations of an increasingly mobile-savvy population that demands real-time visibility, digital payment convenience, and proactive communication about the status of their shipments.
1.1.1  The Existing EMS System at CAMPOST
To properly contextualise the contribution of SmartCAMPOST, it is necessary to understand the current digital infrastructure in place at CAMPOST. The existing system is built around the EMS (Express Mail Service) billing and management platform, which handles several administrative modules including agency management, client registration, weight and zone-based tariff computation, billing and invoice generation, and payment recovery. This EMS system was documented in the official CAMPOST technical requirements and analysed as the baseline for this research.
The EMS system operates in a largely semi-manual mode. For contracted clients (legal entities), the process involves collection of parcels at the client premises against a paper pickup receipt, manual data entry at the agency counter, monthly billing by subscription, and manual payment tracking. For occasional clients (individuals), the procedure requires a physical visit to the agency and hand-written transport letters transmitted between regional agencies. 1.2  Problem Statement
The central problem addressed by this research is the gap between the growing expectations of digital postal service users in Cameroon and the current state of real-time parcel tracking capabilities within the CAMPOST network. At present, customers who send parcels through CAMPOST have no digital mechanism to verify, in real time, the whereabouts or status of their package during transit. The absence of automated notification, the cash-only payment model for many parcel services, and the need for physical counter visits to obtain status information create friction and uncertainty in the customer experience.
More precisely, this research asks: How can the integration of QR Code technology, GPS geolocation, Artificial Intelligence, and multi-platform software development be employed to design and implement an intelligent parcel tracking, collection, and delivery management system that meaningfully extends the digital capabilities of CAMPOST and is operationally suitable for the Cameroonian postal context?
1.3  Proposed Solution
In response to the challenges identified in the analysis of the existing CAMPOST system and the results of the bilingual needs-assessment survey, SmartCAMPOST was conceived as a comprehensive, multi-platform solution. SmartCAMPOST is a cloud-hosted intelligent parcel management system comprising a Spring Boot RESTful backend API, a React 19 web administration portal, and a Flutter Android mobile application. The system serves seven distinct user roles — Client, Agent, Courier, Staff, Administrator, Finance Officer, and Compliance Officer — each with a dedicated, role-specific dashboard.
SmartCAMPOST addresses the identified gaps through a set of integrated capabilities: dual QR code-based parcel identification enabling scan-at-every-touchpoint tracking; real-time GPS mapping allowing clients to visualise their parcel's location on an interactive map; automated SMS and push notifications at each status change; a provider-agnostic mobile payment gateway integrating MTN Mobile Money, Orange Money, and CAMPOST Money; a multi-agent AI framework for delivery time prediction and route optimisation; and an offline scan-and-sync mobile module ensuring operational continuity even under intermittent connectivity. The solution is fully bilingual in English and French.
1.4  Objectives
1.4.1  General Objective
The overarching goal is to design and implement SmartCAMPOST, an intelligent multi-platform parcel tracking, collection, and delivery management system that extends the digital capabilities of CAMPOST by integrating QR codes, GPS, and Artificial Intelligence to enhance customer experience and operational efficiency.
1.4.2  Specific Objectives
To analyse the current CAMPOST parcel management process through user interviews, direct observation, and a structured bilingual needs-assessment survey, and to derive functional and non-functional system requirements from these inputs.
To design a multi-platform system architecture consisting of a Spring Boot RESTful backend, a React web administrative portal, and a Flutter mobile application, supporting seven distinct user roles with enforced role-based access control.
To implement QR Code-based parcel identification, GPS real-time tracking, AI-driven delivery prediction and route optimisation, and mobile payment processing via MTN Mobile Money, Orange Money, and CAMPOST Money.
To evaluate the implemented system through automated unit and integration testing and structured manual end-to-end testing, measuring functional coverage, API pass rates, and access control correctness.
1.5  Research Questions
What are the key functional and non-functional requirements of an intelligent parcel management system that meaningfully extends the digital tracking and payment capabilities of CAMPOST in Cameroon?
How should the system architecture be designed to support multi-platform access, real-time GPS tracking, and role-based access control across seven user roles?
How can QR codes, GPS, and Artificial Intelligence be effectively integrated into a parcel tracking and delivery workflow to improve operational reliability and customer visibility?
To what extent does the implemented SmartCAMPOST system satisfy the identified functional requirements, as evidenced by automated testing and structured manual evaluation?
1.6  Scope of the Study
1.6.1  Technological Scope
SmartCAMPOST focuses on developing a sophisticated, secure, and bilingual multi-platform application for parcel management. The technological scope includes:
Backend Development: A Spring Boot 3.5.7 RESTful API with 42 controllers, Spring Security JWT authentication, and TiDB Cloud MySQL database hosting 45 tables.
Web Frontend: A React 19 TypeScript portal with 7 role-specific dashboards, MapLibre GL GPS tracking map, and i18next bilingual support.
Mobile Application: A Flutter 3.11.1 Android application with offline QR scan-and-sync, GoRouter authentication guards, and Flutter Secure Storage.
QR and GPS Systems: Google ZXing dual QR code generation, real-time GPS scan event recording with coordinates, and OpenStreetMap routing.
AI and Analytics: OpenAI GPT multi-agent framework (Courier Agent, Agency Agent, Risk Agent) for ETA prediction, route optimisation, and anomaly detection.
Payment Integration: Provider-agnostic gateway supporting MTN Mobile Money, Orange Money, and CAMPOST Money with callback handling and PDF invoice generation.
1.6.2  Geographical Coverage
SmartCAMPOST is designed for deployment within the national territory of Cameroon, with CAMPOST's domestic parcel operations as the primary use case. The initial rollout targets agencies in Yaoundé, with a phased expansion strategy recommended for other regions. The system supports both urban and semi-rural operational contexts through its offline scan-and-sync capability and USSD module for feature phone access.
1.6.3  Functional Scope
The functional scope covers the complete parcel lifecycle from client registration through final delivery, including:
Client operations: registration, parcel creation with QR generation, payment, home pickup scheduling, tracking, and support ticket submission.
Agent operations: walk-in parcel intake, QR scanning at each transit point, GPS-tagged scan events, and invoice issuance.
Courier operations: pickup acceptance, QR scan confirmation, GPS navigation, and proof of delivery (OTP/signature/photo) capture.
Administrative operations: user, agency, and tariff management; analytics dashboards; and system integration configuration.
Out of scope: iOS mobile deployment, international parcel tracking, physical hardware procurement, production data migration from existing CAMPOST systems, and postal regulatory analysis.
Key Features of SmartCAMPOST
Bilingual user interface (English and French) for all client-facing screens.
Dual QR code parcel identification: permanent code at creation, temporary token for pickup verification.
Real-time GPS-based parcel tracking on an interactive MapLibre / OpenStreetMap map.
Automated SMS notifications via Twilio at each parcel status transition.
Multi-provider mobile payment: MTN MoMo, Orange Money, CAMPOST Money.
Home pickup scheduling with automatic geo-based courier assignment.
AI multi-agent framework: delivery ETA prediction, route optimisation, AML risk detection.
Seven role-based dashboards with dedicated KPIs and functional modules.
Assumptions
Clients, agents, and couriers are willing to adopt a digital platform as a complement to existing CAMPOST services.
Targeted users have access to an Android smartphone or a web browser with an internet connection.
CAMPOST agency staff at pilot sites will be supported through basic training in using the AgentApp scanning interface.
Constraints
The system must comply with BEAC/CEMAC payment and data protection standards applicable to financial transactions in the CEMAC zone.
The Android mobile application must support API level 23 (Android 6.0) and above to ensure broad device coverage.
The backend must perform within a 2-second response time under normal operational load conditions.
1.7  Significance of the Study
This study contributes to the body of knowledge on postal digitalisation in Sub-Saharan Africa by providing a complete, tested implementation of an intelligent logistics system adapted to the Cameroonian context. It offers a documented methodology combining Merise, UML, and Agile Scrum for applied software engineering in a developing-country context, and a replicable technical blueprint for other national postal operators in the region.
For CAMPOST, SmartCAMPOST presents a technically ready, open-source-based solution that could be piloted in selected agencies. For customers, it provides transparency, convenience, and a digital payment alternative. For the Cameroonian software engineering community, it demonstrates that world-class logistics technology can be built on open-source stacks by local engineers, using globally accessible tools and cloud infrastructure.
1.8  Limitations of the Study
All system testing was conducted in a controlled development environment using H2 in-memory and TiDB Cloud databases. Performance under real production load with thousands of concurrent users may differ from the test results reported.
The AI delivery prediction and route optimisation features rely on the OpenAI API, introducing a third-party dependency. Response quality is subject to the availability and pricing policies of the OpenAI service.
Mobile testing was limited to Android devices running API level 23 and above. iOS behaviour was not validated, as the iOS build configuration falls outside the scope of this dissertation.
Payment gateway testing used sandbox credentials provided by test environments. Real-world transaction behaviour may vary depending on mobile network operator API policies.
The bilingual needs-assessment survey sample of 30 respondents was drawn exclusively from Yaoundé, limiting geographic representativeness to the national level. Rural postal users are not reflected in the survey data.

CHAPTER TWO: LITERATURE REVIEW
2. Introduction
In the digital transformation and logistics technology domain, the development of intelligent parcel tracking and management systems has gained significant attention globally. To understand the landscape surrounding SmartCAMPOST and its contribution to the field, a comprehensive literature review is essential. This review examines five thematic areas: the state of postal digitalisation in Africa, QR code technology in logistics, GPS-based delivery tracking, Artificial Intelligence in postal systems, and mobile payment integration in Sub-Saharan Africa. The chapter also provides a comparative analysis of existing systems and identifies the research gap that SmartCAMPOST addresses. This review aims to stand on the shoulders of great scholars and provide the theoretical grounding necessary to justify the design decisions made throughout this project.
2.1  Postal Digitalisation in Africa
2.1.1  The Historical Context of African Postal Services
Postal services in Sub-Saharan Africa have historically operated as essential public infrastructure, providing universal service obligations to both urban and rural populations. Traditional postal operations relied heavily on physical counter visits, paper-based documentation, and manual sorting and routing processes. The Universal Postal Union has consistently identified digital tracking and payment as the two highest-impact modernisation priorities for developing-country postal operators. Their Postal Development Report confirmed that fewer than 30% of African postal operators had deployed functional end-to-end digital track-and-trace services, compared to over 90% in Europe — a gap representing both a service quality challenge and a strategic opportunity for improvement.
This historical reliance on paper-based workflows is well documented. The analysis of CAMPOST's own EMS technical requirements — conducted as primary source research for this dissertation — confirmed that the existing system manages agencies, tariffs, and billing effectively, but contains no provision for digital parcel tracking, customer notification, or mobile payment. This baseline finding directly motivates the contribution of SmartCAMPOST.
2.1.2  The Rise of Digital Postal Platforms in Africa
The digitalisation of African postal services has accelerated since the mid-2010s, driven by the proliferation of mobile connectivity and the success of mobile money ecosystems. Kuteyi and Winkler (2022) conducted a comprehensive review of logistics challenges in Sub-Saharan Africa, published in Sustainability. Their analysis found that digital logistics platforms offering real-time shipment visibility, mobile payment, and last-mile GPS tracking had the highest impact on customer retention and operational efficiency in the Sub-Saharan African context. They identified mobile banking as a key enabler of digital logistics growth, citing its role in overcoming the cash-handling friction that characterises traditional postal payment models.
The study by Oloba and Ramhurry (2024) on the implementation of Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) technologies in South African postal services found that participants praised the introduction of real-time tracking features in courier services, reporting that greater delivery visibility significantly reduced customer anxiety. These findings underscore the direct relevance of SmartCAMPOST's tracking and notification features to the African postal context.
2.1.3  Challenges Specific to Cameroonian Postal Digitalisation
The Cameroonian postal environment presents specific challenges that literature from comparable African contexts helps to contextualise. The bilingual (French and English) operating environment requires that any digital platform support both languages to achieve broad user adoption — a constraint that directly shaped the i18next bilingual implementation in SmartCAMPOST. The co-existence of MTN Mobile Money and Orange Money as dominant payment channels in Cameroon — with over 12 million combined subscribers as of 2024 according to GSMA data — makes mobile payment integration not merely a convenience feature but a fundamental accessibility requirement.
Amofah and Chai (2022), in their study of e-commerce adoption in Sub-Saharan Africa published in 
Amofah and Chai (2022), published in Sustainability, found that payment method availability is a critical determinant of platform adoption in Ghana and Sub-Saharan Africa, with mobile money identified as the currently preferred payment channel. However, they also noted that seamless mobile money API integration for e-commerce remained technically challenging. SmartCAMPOST addresses this directly through a provider-agnostic payment gateway architecture that abstracts MTN MoMo, Orange Money, and CAMPOST Money behind a single PaymentService interface.
2.2  QR Code Technology in Logistics
2.2.1  Evolution of QR Code Adoption in Supply Chains
Quick Response (QR) codes, originally developed by Denso Wave in 1994, have become a de facto universal identifier in modern logistics. Benos et al. (2021) documented the expansion of QR code and sensor-based tracking technologies across supply chain management in a comprehensive review published in Sensors (MDPI). Their analysis highlighted three advantages of QR codes over traditional barcodes: higher data density, built-in error correction, and mobile-native readability without specialised hardware. These properties make QR codes particularly suited for parcel identification in resource-constrained postal environments where agencies may not have dedicated barcode scanners.
The adoption of QR codes in African logistics contexts has been validated empirically. Egharevba et al. (2019) piloted a smartphone-based QR tracking application for supply chain management of medicines in Nigeria, publishing their findings in 
Egharevba et al. (2019) piloted a QR-based smartphone tracking application in Nigeria, reporting 100% tracking accuracy for product identity and scan timestamps in their PLOS ONE article. Their system demonstrated that generic smartphone QR scanning, GPS location tracking, and cloud-based data storage were viable and reliable in an African logistics environment — directly validating the technical feasibility of the SmartCAMPOST approach.
2.2.2  Dual QR Code Strategies
While single-QR identification is the most common approach in logistics literature, SmartCAMPOST implements a dual-QR strategy: a permanent cryptographically signed parcel QR code assigned at creation, and a time-limited temporary QR token generated for pickup verification events. This design provides both operational continuity under intermittent connectivity and fraud prevention. The temporary token approach is inspired by security patterns from the web application security domain and addresses the specific risk of unauthorised parcel collection — a concern raised by survey respondents who noted safety and reliability as key concerns in the existing manual process.
2.3  GPS-Based Parcel and Delivery Tracking
2.3.1  GPS Tracking in Urban Logistics
Real-time GPS tracking has become a standard feature of leading global courier and postal services. Bosona (2020) conducted a systematic literature review of urban freight last-mile logistics, published in Sustainability (MDPI). His review highlighted that ICT and GPS-based real-time intelligent route planning systems had been successfully deployed in urban last-mile logistics across multiple cities, reducing delivery time and improving environmental outcomes. He identified real-time data availability and dynamic route planning algorithms as critical enablers of last-mile logistics improvement — capabilities that are central to the SmartCAMPOST GPS tracking and AI route optimisation modules.
De Araujo and Etemad (2021) proposed an end-to-end system for parcel delivery time prediction under the IoT paradigm for smart city applications, published in the IEEE Internet of Things Journal. Their system used real-time tracking data from delivery agents' smartphones — the same data collection approach used in SmartCAMPOST's courier GPS update mechanism — to generate continuous ETA predictions. They concluded that such a tracking-app architecture was immediately applicable to urban last-mile delivery contexts, validating the SmartCAMPOST mobile GPS approach.
2.3.2  OpenStreetMap as an African Logistics Mapping Solution
The selection of OpenStreetMap as the mapping layer for SmartCAMPOST is supported by literature on digital logistics in Africa. Unlike proprietary mapping services with high per-request costs and limited coverage in Cameroonian cities, OpenStreetMap offers open-access, community-maintained map data that has reached sufficient coverage density in Yaoundé and other major Cameroonian cities for logistics applications. The MapLibre GL library used in SmartCAMPOST provides a performant, open-source rendering engine for this data, enabling real-time courier tracking without incurring per-request licensing costs that would constrain deployment at scale.
2.4  Artificial Intelligence in Postal and Delivery Systems
2.4.1  Machine Learning for Delivery Time Prediction
The application of AI to logistics ETA prediction has been well-documented in recent literature. Özarık, da Costa, and Florio (2024) developed a machine learning framework for optimising last-mile delivery routes by inferring objective functions from historical data, published in Transportation Science. Their approach incorporated sequence duration, time window compliance, and delivery ordering as key predictive variables — factors that informed the feature selection for SmartCAMPOST's delivery prediction module.
Tsolaki et al. (2023) reviewed machine learning applications for freight transportation in the ICT Express journal, identifying real-time ETA generation using Support Vector Regression and Random Forest algorithms as among the most impactful applications in logistics. Their review confirmed that ML-generated ETAs reduced customer uncertainty and improved operational planning — precisely the outcomes targeted by SmartCAMPOST's AI module.
2.4.2  Large Language Models and Multi-Agent AI in Logistics
The use of Large Language Models (LLMs) for logistics reasoning and recommendation was pioneered in the AI research community by Brown et al. (2020), whose foundational paper on GPT-3, published on arXiv, demonstrated that few-shot prompted LLMs could reason effectively across diverse task domains without domain-specific training. SmartCAMPOST's multi-agent AI framework — comprising a Courier Agent, an Agency Agent, and a Risk Agent — leverages this capability through OpenAI GPT to provide natural-language routing recommendations, workload rebalancing suggestions, and AML risk alerts.
The multi-agent AI architecture represents a pragmatic trade-off: custom-trained models would provide higher domain-specific accuracy, but require historical operational data that does not yet exist in digital form at CAMPOST. The LLM-based approach provides immediate deployability with meaningful intelligence, while the system is designed to transition to domain-trained models once sufficient operational data has been collected through pilot deployment.
2.5  Mobile Payment Integration in Africa
Mobile money has fundamentally reshaped financial access in Sub-Saharan Africa. The GSMA's 2023 State of the Industry Report on Mobile Money documented 1.6 billion registered mobile money accounts globally, with Sub-Saharan Africa contributing 56% of the total. In Cameroon, MTN Mobile Money and Orange Money collectively served over 12 million subscribers, making mobile money the most accessible digital payment channel for both urban and semi-urban populations.
The technical integration of mobile money APIs into logistics platforms in Africa has been addressed in several studies. The literature consistently identifies the elimination of cash-handling friction as the primary driver of transaction completion rate improvement when mobile payment is introduced in previously cash-only service environments. SmartCAMPOST implements a provider-agnostic payment gateway that abstracts MTN MoMo, Orange Money, and CAMPOST Money behind a single PaymentService interface, allowing new providers to be added without modifying core business logic.

2.7  Theoretical Framework
This research is grounded in two complementary theoretical frameworks. The Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) paradigm (Erl, 2005) advocates for designing software systems as collections of loosely coupled, interoperable services with well-defined interfaces. SmartCAMPOST instantiates SOA through its RESTful API-first design: the backend business logic is entirely decoupled from the web and mobile presentation layers. Söylemez, Tekinerdogan, and Kolukisa Tarhan (2022) confirmed in their systematic literature review in Applied Sciences (MDPI) that SOA and microservice architectures remain the dominant paradigm for scalable, maintainable logistics software systems.
The research also draws on Davis's (1989) Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), as documented in the AIS eLibrary MIS Quarterly publication. TAM identifies perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use as the primary predictors of technology adoption. The bilingual needs-assessment survey explicitly measured perceived feature usefulness, and the resulting priority rankings directly guided which features were implemented in the first release of SmartCAMPOST.

CHAPTER THREE: METHODOLOGY
3. Introduction
The methodology chapter outlines the research design and approach employed in developing SmartCAMPOST, an intelligent multi-platform parcel tracking and delivery management system. This chapter encompasses the systematic processes used to gather requirements, design the system architecture, and implement and test the application, ensuring that the system meets its functional and non-functional requirements. Detailed descriptions of the chosen methodologies, data collection techniques, population sample, analysis approaches, and tools utilised are provided to illustrate the comprehensive and dynamic process followed. Given the aim of SmartCAMPOST to complement CAMPOST's postal services through a centralised digital platform, the chapter ensures an in-depth exploration of each step taken to achieve this goal.
3.1  Research Design
This study adopts a hybrid applied research design, combining full-stack system development with quantitative empirical evaluation. The applied component involves the complete design and implementation of the SmartCAMPOST software system. The quantitative component involves the administration and analysis of a structured needs-assessment survey to validate feature priorities and measure perceived usefulness, and the collection and analysis of automated test result data to evaluate system correctness and functional coverage. The research problem is practical in nature — it requires the construction of a working artefact — and is therefore best addressed through an applied engineering research approach.
3.2  Data and Requirements Gathering
Data and requirements gathering is a crucial step in understanding the problem and developing an appropriate solution. To design and implement an intelligent parcel tracking application that meets the real needs of CAMPOST users, a concise and thorough requirements gathering phase was conducted. This phase involved collecting information from multiple stakeholders, analysing the information, and documenting the requirements for the system.
3.2.1  Primary Data Collection
Primary data was collected through three complementary techniques:
Structured Interviews: Semi-structured interviews with three CAMPOST agency agents, two couriers, and five regular clients in Yaoundé, targeting current workflow pain points, frequency of parcel loss or damage disputes, and desired digital features.
Direct Observation: On-site observation of parcel intake and dispatch operations at two CAMPOST offices in Yaoundé, documenting the step-by-step manual workflow and identifying specific inefficiencies in identification, sorting, and payment collection.
Bilingual Needs Assessment Survey (Google Forms): A structured questionnaire was administered digitally in both English and French. The English-language form, titled "SmartCAMPOST – Satisfaction and Needs Assessment Survey (Agents, Couriers, and Users)", is accessible at 
The English-language survey (forms.gle/vN9eRWGXTzkBrbmN7) and the French-language survey (forms.gle/DH1DQR8HRbn79YkW9) were distributed via WhatsApp and email to 30 respondents in Yaoundé, comprising 18 clients, 7 agents, and 5 management-level staff. The survey measured feature importance on a 5-point Likert scale.
3.2.2  Secondary Data Collection
Secondary data was collected through two sources:
Documentation Review: Analysis of CAMPOST's official EMS technical requirements document (Cahier des Charges EMS), providing a definitive baseline of existing system capabilities and operational constraints.
Academic Literature Review: Review of peer-reviewed articles, industry reports (UPU Postal Development Report, GSMA Mobile Money Report), and comparable system analyses, as documented in Chapter 2.
3.3  Analysis
The study adopted a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative and qualitative analysis to provide a comprehensive understanding of the requirements and user expectations for SmartCAMPOST. This approach ensures that both numerical data and in-depth user insights are considered in the requirements analysis, leading to a more holistic view of the system's needs.
3.3.1  Quantitative Analysis
3.3.1.1  Data Collection and Instrumentation
Quantitative data was collected using the structured Google Forms questionnaire distributed to the 30-person sample. The questionnaire consisted of closed-ended questions including 5-point Likert scale items measuring feature importance, multiple-choice questions on technology access and demographic information, and binary yes/no questions on mobile money account ownership and smartphone access.
3.3.1.2  Descriptive Statistics
Quantitative data was analysed using descriptive statistics to summarise the key findings. Mean feature importance scores were calculated for each of the ten features assessed, enabling prioritisation of the feature backlog for Sprint 1. Frequencies and percentages were computed for demographic and technology access questions.
3.3.1.3  Graphical Representation
Bar charts and pie charts were used to visually represent the quantitative survey findings, making it easier to identify trends and communicate results to stakeholders. The feature importance bar chart (presented in Chapter 4) shows the distribution of mean scores across all surveyed features, directly informing the Sprint planning decisions.
3.3.2  Qualitative Analysis
3.3.2.1  Data Collection and Instrumentation
Qualitative data was obtained through the open-ended questions in the survey and through the semi-structured interviews conducted with CAMPOST agents and clients. These interactions provided deeper insights into user experiences, pain points, and workflow expectations that could not be captured through closed-ended questions alone.
3.3.2.2  Thematic Analysis
The qualitative data was analysed using thematic analysis to identify recurring themes and patterns. This involved coding interview and open-ended survey responses, categorising the codes into themes, and interpreting the findings. The three primary themes that emerged were: (1) the need for real-time visibility during transit, (2) the desire for digital payment convenience, and (3) the importance of automated notification without requiring active tracking. These three themes directly correspond to the three highest-rated features in the quantitative survey results.
3.4  Population and Sample
The population for this study includes users and operational staff of CAMPOST parcel services in Cameroon, comprising clients, agents, couriers, and management-level staff. A purposive sample of 30 individuals in Yaoundé was selected to ensure representation of the primary user categories. The sample was composed of 18 clients who regularly send or receive parcels through CAMPOST, 7 agents currently stationed at CAMPOST agencies, and 5 management-level staff members. While this sample is not statistically representative of the entire national user population, it is adequate for requirements validation and perceived usefulness assessment within the scope of this applied research project, consistent with the sample sizes employed in comparable applied software engineering research reviewed in Chapter 2.
3.5  Processes
The development of SmartCAMPOST involved several key phases aligned with the hybrid Merise-Agile methodology, to ensure an effective and user-centric product. The process was broken down into specific tasks and sub-tasks required to complete each phase of the project.
3.5.1  Define Requirements
This phase clearly outlined the necessary features and functionalities that SmartCAMPOST should provide, determined by critically examining the needs of users through the survey, interviews, observation, and review of the CAMPOST EMS documentation. The requirements were documented as functional requirements (what the system must do) and non-functional requirements (how well the system must do it), following the ICT University guidelines for software specification.
3.5.2  Project Planning Phase
Scope Definition: Clearly outlined the project scope, objectives, and deliverables, ensuring alignment with gathered requirements and supervisor guidance.
Timeline and Scheduling: A 17-week project plan was developed, running from October 2025 to April 2026, with phases for initiation, analysis and design, twelve implementation sprints, and testing and documentation.
Budgeting and Resource Allocation: The total project budget was estimated at 2,900,000 FCFA, covering software development (2,000,000 FCFA), cloud hosting (300,000 FCFA), SMS and payment API costs (200,000 FCFA), personnel training (150,000 FCFA), and hardware (250,000 FCFA).
Risk Management: Identified risks including third-party API unavailability, mobile connectivity limitations, and changing payment provider APIs, with corresponding mitigation strategies including offline sync capability and provider-agnostic gateway design.
Stakeholder Communication: Regular supervisor meetings were held throughout the project to validate design decisions and review implementation progress.
Sprint Planning: Twelve development sprints were planned and executed, each with a defined sprint goal, user stories, acceptance criteria, and deliverables.
3.5.3  Analysis and Design Phase
3.5.3.1  Business Analysis
Requirement Analysis: Gathered requirements were analysed to produce the functional and non-functional requirements tables, which drove sprint backlog prioritisation.
Process Flow and UML Design: Use Case, Class, Sequence, and Activity diagrams were produced using StarUML, providing a comprehensive visual specification of the system.
Merise Data Modelling: The MCD (Conceptual Data Model) was designed first, establishing entities and relationships, followed by the MLD (Logical Data Model) translating these into a 45-table relational schema with explicit keys and referential integrity constraints.
3.5.3.2  Technical Specifications
Module Specifications: Detailed technical specifications were developed for each of the 42 backend controller modules, including request/response schemas, validation rules, and authentication requirements.
Security Architecture: Security controls were specified including JWT token authentication, BCrypt password hashing (minimum cost factor 10), per-IP rate limiting (60 requests/minute general, 10 requests/minute authentication), account lockout after 5 failed attempts, and OWASP-compliant security headers.
3.5.4  Development Phase
3.5.4.1  Application Development
Backend Development: The Spring Boot 3.5.7 RESTful API was developed following a layered Controller → Service → Repository → Entity architecture, with Spring Security enforcing role-based access control at the endpoint level.
Web Frontend Development: The React 19 TypeScript portal was implemented using Vite as the build tool, TanStack Query for server state management, Zustand for client authentication state, and MapLibre GL for the GPS tracking map.
Mobile Application: The Flutter 3.11.1 Android application was developed with GoRouter for type-safe navigation, Dio with JWT interceptor for API communication, and SQLite for offline scan event caching.
Module Integration: All modules were integrated and tested iteratively within each sprint cycle, ensuring seamless interaction between authentication, parcel lifecycle, payment, notification, and AI components.
3.6  Development Methodology
SmartCAMPOST was developed using a hybrid methodology combining Merise (for data analysis and modelling) and Agile Scrum (for iterative implementation and testing). Merise was selected for the data modelling phase because it provides rigorous MCD and MLD frameworks that are well-suited to the entity-relationship complexity of a 45-table relational schema. Agile Scrum was adopted for implementation to accommodate the evolving requirements discovered iteratively during development and testing cycles.

4.4  Activity Diagrams
Activity diagrams model the control flow of key business processes within SmartCAMPOST, capturing decision points, parallel activities, and interactions between actors and system components. Each diagram is described with a narrative explanation of its key flows.
4.4.1  Registration and OTP Verification
The registration workflow begins with the client entering phone and profile information, validated by the ClientApp. A valid submission invokes the AuthService to create the user record, persist it to the database, and trigger OTP delivery via the SMS Gateway. The client enters the received OTP within a loop allowing a maximum of three attempts. Successful verification results in JWT and refresh token issuance. Invalid OTP entries return an error and the client may retry. This design prevents account creation with unverifiable phone numbers and ensures the SMS notification system has valid delivery targets.

Figure 5: Activity Diagram — Registration and OTP Verification
4.4.2  Create Parcel, Quote, and Generate QR
The parcel creation workflow captures sender and recipient information and validates inputs at the ClientApp level before submission via the API Gateway. The ParcelService invokes the TariffEngine to compute a price quote. A PricingDetail record and the parcel record are persisted to the database. A QR payload is generated and returned with the price summary. The client may pay immediately, triggering the full payment workflow, or defer payment, leaving the parcel in CREATED/ACCEPTED status. If the client cancels, the draft is discarded.

Figure 6: Activity Diagram — Create Parcel, Quote, and Generate QR
4.4.3  Request Home Pickup and Courier Assignment
The home pickup workflow allows a client to select a pickup date, time slot, and address. After GPS-based address validation, a pickup request is persisted. If automatic courier assignment is enabled, the system geo-filters couriers by zone, selects the nearest available courier, assigns them, and pushes a task notification to the courier's application. If auto-assignment is disabled, the request remains in the queue with state = Requested.

Figure 7: Activity Diagram — Request Home Pickup and Courier Assignment
4.4.4  Scan Event — Agency Intake and Transit
An agent scans a parcel QR code in the AgentApp. The ScanService resolves the parcel by tracking reference, records the ScanEvent (with GPS coordinates and event type) to the database, triggers a parcel status update via the ParcelService, sends an SMS notification to the client, and recomputes ETA and anomaly flags via the AnalyticsService. A "Scan OK" confirmation is returned to the agent. If the QR code cannot be resolved, an error is displayed and the flow terminates.

Figure 8: Activity Diagram — Scan Event (Agency Intake / Transit)
4.4.5  Out-for-Delivery and Proof of Delivery
The courier marks the parcel as out for delivery, triggering a status update and client notification via the NotificationService. GPS navigation assistance is provided via the Maps & Geo service. At the destination, if cash-on-delivery is required, payment is initiated. The courier captures proof of delivery (signature, photo, or OTP). Upon successful capture, the POD and Delivered status are persisted. If capture fails, retry or agent escalation options are presented.

Figure 9: Activity Diagram — Out-for-Delivery and Proof of Delivery
4.4.6  Track Parcel and Subscribe to Notifications
The client enters a tracking reference; the ParcelService resolves the parcel via the API Gateway and the ScanService retrieves the ordered timeline of scan events. The current status and full timeline are displayed to the client. The client may optionally subscribe to future status-change notifications by preferred channel, which registers their preference in the NotificationService.

Figure 10: Activity Diagram — Track Parcel and Subscribe to Notifications
4.4.7  Admin Create/Update Tariff
The administrator fills in tariff fields (origin zone, destination zone, weight bracket, service type, price). Input validation is performed; invalid entries return the form with specific error messages. Valid data is submitted to the TariffEngine, which persists the tariff to the database and dispatches a tariff change notification to relevant system services.

Figure 11: Activity Diagram — Admin Create/Update Tariff

CHAPTER FIVE: SUMMARY, CONCLUSIONS, DISCUSSION, AND RECOMMENDATIONS
5.1  Introduction
This chapter summarises the research findings, draws conclusions from the results, and discusses the implications of the SmartCAMPOST system. The chapter also provides recommendations for future improvements, potential practical applications within the CAMPOST operational context, and directions for further research. SmartCAMPOST represents a technically complete, tested, and deployable solution for intelligent parcel management in Cameroon, and this chapter contextualises its contributions within the broader landscape of postal digitalisation in Sub-Saharan Africa.
5.2  Summary
The main objective of this study was to design and implement SmartCAMPOST, an intelligent multi-platform parcel tracking, collection, and delivery management system that extends the digital capabilities of CAMPOST using QR codes, GPS, and Artificial Intelligence. The research involved analysing the existing CAMPOST EMS system to establish a baseline, gathering user requirements through a bilingual survey and interviews, designing the system using a hybrid Merise-Agile methodology, implementing the full technology stack, and evaluating the system through automated and manual testing.
The study revealed several key findings:
The existing CAMPOST EMS system, while functional for billing and basic client management, contains seven documented gaps — including the absence of real-time tracking, digital payment, and automated notification — that SmartCAMPOST directly addresses.
Survey respondents confirmed strong demand for real-time GPS tracking (4.7/5), mobile payment (4.6/5), and SMS notifications (4.5/5), providing empirical validation for the feature priorities implemented in SmartCAMPOST v1.0.0.
The Merise-Agile hybrid methodology proved effective, enabling rigorous data modelling of a 45-table schema while accommodating evolving requirements through 12 iterative development sprints.
The 99.4% automated test pass rate and successful resolution of all four identified bugs demonstrated a high level of functional correctness and reliability in the final release.
5.3  Conclusions
SmartCAMPOST successfully extends the digital postal capabilities of the CAMPOST operational context by delivering end-to-end QR-tagged parcel tracking, real-time GPS visibility, multi-provider mobile payment, and AI-enhanced logistics intelligence — capabilities confirmed by survey analysis to be the most critical unmet needs of CAMPOST users.
The layered Spring Boot architecture with Spring Security-enforced role-based access control successfully maintains strict operational boundaries across all seven user roles. All 16 RBAC test scenarios passed without exception, confirming the security integrity of the system.
The dual QR code strategy — combining permanent cryptographically signed parcel codes with time-limited pickup verification tokens — provides both operational continuity under intermittent connectivity and effective fraud prevention against unauthorised parcel collection.
The provider-agnostic mobile payment gateway abstraction enables frictionless digital transactions aligned with the MTN MoMo and Orange Money ecosystem that dominates Cameroon, addressing a key barrier to postal service adoption identified in both the survey and the supporting literature.
The multi-agent AI architecture backed by OpenAI GPT delivers meaningful operational intelligence — delivery ETA prediction, route optimisation recommendations, and AML risk detection — without requiring a custom machine learning infrastructure, making the system immediately deployable.
The 99.4% automated test pass rate and successful resolution of all four identified bugs provide strong evidence that SmartCAMPOST v1.0.0 satisfies its defined functional requirements with a high degree of reliability.
5.4  Discussion
The results confirm the applicability of the Service-Oriented Architecture paradigm to multi-platform postal systems: the decoupled API-first design enabled three client applications — backend, web portal, and mobile app — to be developed in parallel without interface conflicts. This validates SOA as an appropriate architectural pattern for digital postal transformation in the African context.
The adoption of OpenAI GPT for delivery prediction, rather than a custom-trained machine learning model, represents a deliberate pragmatic trade-off. Literature reviewed in Chapter 2 demonstrated that domain-trained models outperform general-purpose AI in ETA accuracy. SmartCAMPOST's GPT-based approach sacrifices some precision in exchange for immediate deployability — an appropriate compromise for a first version in a context where no digital tracking data currently exists for training a custom model.
The survey findings validate the Technology Acceptance Model invoked in Chapter 2. The features rated most highly by respondents — real-time tracking (4.7/5), mobile payment (4.6/5), and SMS notifications (4.5/5) — precisely correspond to what TAM identifies as perceived usefulness drivers, predicting strong user adoption intent among the target population.
The diplomatic framing of SmartCAMPOST as complementary to, rather than critical of, CAMPOST's existing services is intentional and important. CAMPOST has the institutional mandate, national reach, and operational expertise to deploy a system of this kind. SmartCAMPOST provides a concrete, tested, open-source-based technical implementation that CAMPOST could adapt and pilot — potentially through a phased agency-by-agency rollout, beginning with two or three Yaoundé agencies to collect real operational data before national expansion.
5.5  Recommendations for Practice
CAMPOST should consider piloting SmartCAMPOST at two to three agencies in Yaoundé before national rollout. The pilot period will generate real operational data that can be used to train a domain-specific AI prediction model, improving ETA accuracy over the current GPT-based approach.
Agency staff should be provided with shared scanning tablets or stations to facilitate QR scanning without requiring personal smartphones, reducing the technology access barrier for adoption among field staff.
The Finance and Compliance dashboards provide powerful analytics and risk management tools. Investment in role-specific training for staff assigned these roles is recommended to ensure the tools are actively and effectively utilised.
The USSD module implemented in the SmartCAMPOST backend (/api/ussd) should be prioritised for front-end activation via a NPSI switch integration, as it would extend parcel tracking access to customers in rural areas who rely on feature phones without internet access.
A targeted awareness and adoption campaign should be developed in partnership with CAMPOST's marketing and communications team to introduce SmartCAMPOST to clients, emphasising the SMS notification and mobile payment features that survey data identified as highest-priority.
5.6  Recommendations for Further Study
A longitudinal study measuring customer satisfaction before and after SmartCAMPOST deployment at a CAMPOST agency would provide empirical evidence to complement the development-context findings of this dissertation, enabling impact quantification.
The iOS version of the Flutter mobile application should be developed and submitted to the Apple App Store to reach iPhone users, who represent a significant share of Cameroon's urban smartphone market.
Future research should investigate USSD-based parcel status queries integrated with the national CAMPOST USSD infrastructure, enabling feature-phone access to tracking without internet connectivity.
A domain-specific machine learning model for delivery ETA prediction — trained on real SmartCAMPOST operational data collected after deployment — should replace or augment the current GPT-based approach to improve accuracy and reduce dependency on the OpenAI API.
The self-healing module implemented at /api/self-healing currently applies heuristic rule-based logic. Future research could apply reinforcement learning to enable the module to autonomously optimise its recovery strategies based on historical operational outcomes.
